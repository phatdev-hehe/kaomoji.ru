<h1>Kaomoji: Japanese Emoticons</h1>
<p><strong>Kaomoji</strong> (顔文字) is a popular Japanese emoticon style made up of Japanese characters and grammar punctuations, and are used to express emotion in texting and cyber communication. The word <b>kaomoji</b> is also synonymous to be referred to as <b>Japanese emoticons</b>. This concept is formed by the combination of two words in Kanji, “kao” (顔 – “face”) and “moji” (文字 – “character”).</p>
<p>Japanese are very emotional and creative nation. Therefore, in Japan emoticons are popular as nowhere in the world.</p>
<p>Japanese believe that eyes are the mirror of a human soul. Therefore, unlike Western emoticons where most attention is paid to mouth, in <b>Japanese emoticons</b> the most important part are eyes. In addition, <b>kaomoji</b> are so popular because you don’t have to read them sideways.</p>
<p>Many Japanese are good at drawing because Japanese is the language of drawings. Anime and manga are vivid examples of how closely the authors manage to convey a variety of emotions just by a set of a few simple lines.</p>
<p>In the beginning of internet and instant messaging – lack of face-to-face talk resulted in mistakenly taking parodies and humorous jokes the wrong way. <b>Kaomoji</b> is a result of manga and anime fans who wanted to correct those mistakes, avoid awkward writing situations, and express words more creatively.</p>
<p><strong>Japanese emoticons</strong> are extremely diverse. Some of Internet sources state the number of 10000 but, in fact, there are much more of them. Such diversity may be explained by at least two factors:
<ul>
<li>unlike Cyrillic and Latin alphabets, which are usually written in single-byte character sets, Japanese writing requires a minimum of double-byte sets with a wider scope of characters;</li>
<li><b>kaomoji</b> can express not only individual emotions, but also complex actions, objects, and even whole stories.</li>
</ul>
</p>
<p><b>Japanese emoticons</b> are divided into categories based on emotional component of a form, action or object. These emoticons can also use some special characters for additional connotation.</p> 
<p>On this website you can find an interesting collection of kawaii (cute) <b>Japanese emoji</b> compiled from various (mostly Japanese) internet sources. Since it is impossible to find and display all of them, we selected the most interesting and cute ones, as well as added our own. In addition, we also developed an application for Android.</p>
<p><a name="contents"></a>So! Feel free to use these <b>Japanese emoticons</b>. Mix and match the elements of different <b>kaomoji</b> and create your own creative options!</p>

<h3><a name="joy">Joy</a></h3>
<p>If you want to create joyful, happy or laughing Japanese emoticons use high up eyes. For example, you can use these characters: ^, ￣, ´ and `. Mouths are important too. Japanese girls often use character ω (omega) for mouths of their emoticons, thinking that such kaomoji are cute, or, as they say, kawaii. You can also use ∀, ▽ and other characters resembling a smile. And Japanese also like to add different effects to kaomoji (stars, tears of joy, and so on) for better emphasis.</p>

<h3><a name="love">Love</a></h3>
<p>Love and kissing Japanese emoji often use character ♡ (heart) or its combination, for example, ノ～ ♡ (air kiss). You can also use a set of characters "chu" for a kiss (in Japan it means a sound of a kiss). Another interesting love emoticons feature is an active presence of such characters as * or o, which mean "rousy cheeks" and are often used in combination with /, \, ノ, ﾉ and ヽ. This emoticons are trying to hide their faces in embarrassment. You can also use character ε (bow lips), which indicates the desire to hug and kiss. Japanese girls often joke saying that such kaomoji look like perverts!</p>
